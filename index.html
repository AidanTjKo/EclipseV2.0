üìúHistory of Eclipses

Throughout history, eclipses have cast a spellbinding shadow across human cultures. Ancient civilizations saw them as cosmic dramas, where celestial beings battled for supremacy.

The Babylonians recorded their appearances, seeking patterns in the sky's enigmatic dance.

Today, eclipses continue to captivate us, not as omens or auguries, but as awe-inspiring reminders of the vast, interconnected cosmos in which we reside.

‚è∞ When do eclipses occur?

Eclipses, both solar and lunar, are celestial phenomena that occur with remarkable regularity, yet their precise timing and locations remain a testament to the precision of scientific prediction.

On average, there are two to four solar and two to four lunar eclipses each year, but the exact number can vary.

This predictability arises from the meticulous calculations of astronomers who rely on complex mathematical models that take into account the orbits of the Earth, Moon, and Sun.

Through these calculations, scientists can forecast eclipse occurrences down to the minute and predict where they will be visible on Earth's surface, allowing enthusiasts and scientists alike to witness these captivating cosmic events with pinpoint accuracy.
